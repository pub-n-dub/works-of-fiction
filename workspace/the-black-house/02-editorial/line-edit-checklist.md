# Line-Edit Checklist

This checklist turns the editorial memo into repeatable line-level decisions. The examples below come from the current working manuscript in `01-manuscript/the-black-house-working.md`.

## How To Use This Checklist

When editing, do not try to preserve every original sentence shape. The priority is to preserve tone, image, and psychic pressure while reducing repetition, over-explanation, and rhetorical drag.

For each flagged passage, ask:

1. Has the image already done the work?
2. Is the sentence explaining a feeling the scene already implies?
3. Is the same emotional note being struck twice?
4. Can the sentence become sharper by removing qualifiers or commentary?

## 1. Trim Self-Explanation After Strong Imagery

One recurring habit is to build a vivid image, then explain its emotional meaning in the next sentence. Often the explanation can be cut or shortened.

### Example

From `Prelude`:

> "I do not know what to think or believe, but I do know that I am continually haunted by demons of the night."

This line is not weak by itself, but it comes after a sequence already establishing uncertainty and dream-haunting. The passage may not need such direct thematic declaration so early.

### Editing Goal

- Keep the image or scene
- Cut the sentence that names the effect too explicitly
- Trust dread to register without commentary

## 2. Reduce Repeated Abstract Framing

The manuscript often uses sentences that announce thought, belief, remembrance, or significance before the concrete material arrives.

### Example

From `Prelude`:

> "I felt that I should try to communicate that part of me that is seldom discussed."

### Example

From `Prelude`:

> "I feel I have covered everything needed to understand what I am about to reveal."

These are framing sentences. They slow momentum and tell the reader how to receive the material.

### Editing Goal

- Remove throat-clearing
- Enter the event sooner
- Keep reflective framing only when it adds voice rather than delay

## 3. Compress "I knew" Constructions

`I knew` is one of the manuscript's most repeated sentence engines. Sometimes it works as obsession or certainty. Often it becomes padding.

### Example

From `Prelude`:

> "I knew the place, as if I had lived there in a previous life, but, of course, this is all in my mind."

### Example

From `Absolution`:

> "All I knew was that I had a..."

### Example

From later in the manuscript:

> "I knew something was wrong when I found in my pocket, one of the figurines from the cage in the attic."

### Editing Goal

- Keep `I knew` only when certainty itself is dramatically important
- Otherwise, state the perception directly
- Watch for clusters of `I knew` in close proximity

## 4. Watch for Overuse of "As If"

`As if` appears constantly across the collection. It is useful for dream logic, but too much of it weakens the prose by making every impression approximate.

### Example

From `Prelude`:

> "As if I had been paraded down this eerie hall in some earlier time."

### Example

From `Absolution`:

> "I felt as if my soul were wrapped in a warm blanket."

### Example

From `The Electric Cipher`:

> "It was as if the knowledge of Frank had been implanted..."

### Editing Goal

- Keep the best `as if` constructions
- Replace weaker ones with direct perception
- Do not let every uncanny feeling arrive through the same mechanism

## 5. Cut Qualifiers That Blur Intensity

Words like `quite`, `really`, `very`, `perhaps`, and `seemed` often dilute sentences that want to be forceful.

### Example

From `Prelude`:

> "this vision was quite perplexing"

### Example

From `A Forgotten Memory`:

> "it was a scream that was very..."

### Example

From `The Electric Cipher`:

> "I don't know, had some disturbing dreams last night, can't really..."

### Editing Goal

- Delete qualifiers first and reread
- Add force through noun, verb, and image, not through adverbs
- Leave a qualifier in only if uncertainty is the point

## 6. Simplify Melodramatic Intensifiers

Some passages stack intensity terms so heavily that they blur together rather than sharpen.

### Example

From `Prelude`:

> "Moans of such a magnitude that the dusty sound rattled the bones in my ears and pricked the hairs upon my neck."

### Example

From `The Flashback`:

> "I was blackened, a soul of ash, empty, abandoned, ready to strike and sate my desires."

These may be close to effective, but the line edit should test whether every intensifier is earning its place.

### Editing Goal

- Preserve the strongest image in the sentence
- Cut the second or third phrase if they are doing the same work
- Favor precision over accumulation

## 7. Tighten Explanatory Dialogue

Dialogue sometimes functions as exposition rather than speech. Characters say what the story needs explained instead of what a person would naturally say.

### Example

From `Absolution`:

> "The devil has gotten you! If only you had followed through with your oath! God has punished you, God has forsaken you! You're at the mercy of the devil!"

This may fit the character, but it should be checked against the tonal threshold of the scene. If everyone speaks at maximum pitch, escalation loses value.

### Editing Goal

- Preserve stylization, but vary speech pressure
- Let subtext replace declaration where possible
- Reduce speeches that summarize theme too neatly

## 8. Cut Repeated Emotional Conclusions

The manuscript often states a reaction, then restates it in slightly different language.

### Example

From `Prelude`:

> "I wish I could illustrate my images fully, but I have trouble describing them, since they are clear in my mind but not so when I put them on paper."

### Example

From `Absolution`:

> "I wish I could be away from them, I wish I could wish them away. Then things would be okay, I know it."

The voice may justify some repetition, but line editing should test whether both halves are needed.

### Editing Goal

- Keep the first strong formulation
- Remove the echoed formulation unless repetition is rhythmic and deliberate
- Be especially alert when two clauses mean nearly the same thing

## 9. Prefer Concrete Scene Over Philosophical Setup

Many openings and transitions move first through abstraction, then arrive at the scene. Often the scene should come earlier.

### Example

From `Prelude`:

> "What qualifies a vision? What makes it what it is? What could possibly be its composition?"

This sets tone, but it also delays narrative entry. The question is not whether to cut all abstraction, but whether so much is needed before the first concrete image.

### Editing Goal

- Bring forward place, body, object, weather, or action
- Keep philosophical lines only when they intensify voice
- Use abstraction as contrast, not as default mode

## 10. Normalize Mechanical or Conversion Artifacts During Edit

Some issues are not stylistic so much as textual cleanup.

### Check For

- title capitalization inconsistencies
- spacing oddities
- ellipses and em dash consistency
- converted markup or residual formatting
- repeated front matter elements from the source file

### Example

The current working extract still carries source-era front matter and formatting remnants from conversion. These should be cleaned before final production.

## Suggested Editing Pass Order

1. Remove duplicated or unnecessary framing sentences
2. Cut `I knew` / `as if` / qualifier clutter
3. Tighten dialogue that over-explains
4. Recheck paragraph rhythm after cuts
5. Proof style consistency only after line edits are stable

## Definition Of A Good Edit For This Book

A successful line edit should make the manuscript:

- sharper without making it ordinary
- stranger without making it vague
- darker without making it overwritten
- cleaner without stripping away voice
