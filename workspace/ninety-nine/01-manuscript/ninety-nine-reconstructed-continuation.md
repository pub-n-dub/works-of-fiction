# Ninety-Nine Reconstructed Continuation

This is a modern reconstruction draft intended to follow immediately
after the current stopping point of `ninety-nine-working.md`. It is not
claimed as recovered source text. It is written as an integration-ready
continuation for editorial use.

---

	May 13.  I waited all day for Heather to call and she never did.  The
phone sat on the table with a dumb, heavy silence about it, the kind of
silence that makes you feel mocked by an inanimate object.  I smoked too
much and stared at the typewriter.  Rhindy came home from work in a
better mood than she had been in all week; she smiled when she saw me
still there as if my mere presence in the apartment should have been
construed as fidelity.  I could not decide which was worse—her anger or
her good humor.  At least when she was furious there was no confusion.

	“You stayed home tonight,” she said.

	“For now.”

	She put her purse on the chair, kicked off her shoes, and went to the
refrigerator.  “For now?”  She glanced at me over the door.  “What’s
that supposed to mean?”

	“Nothing.  It means I’m here.”

	“That’s not an answer.”

	I lit another cigarette and stared at the curl of smoke.  “I’m tired,
Rhindy.”

	She looked at me as if trying to decide whether I was being dramatic or
just cruel.  “You’re always tired.”

	“Maybe because I’m tired of this.”

	“This?”

	I waved my hand at the walls, the couch, the dishes in the sink, the
heat trapped in the little rooms, the whole absurd domestic theater of
our life together.  “This.”

	She shut the refrigerator and stood with her back against it.  “Then
leave.”

	There it was.  Not said in rage this time or in the heat of an
argument, but coolly, with the full force of practical meaning.  The
words sounded clean in the room.  I could feel my heart beating
faster—not from fear exactly, but from that awful thrill you get when
something you’ve talked about so often finally steps out of abstraction.

	“I’m going to.”

	She laughed once, a small humorless exhalation.  “No, you’re not.  You
always say that.  You say that, then you go out, then you come back, and
then we do this all over again.”

	“I said I’m going to.”

	She narrowed her eyes.  “Do you have somewhere to go?”

	“Yes.”

	“With who?”

	I did not answer.

	Her jaw tightened.  “So there is somebody.”

	“There is always somebody, Rhindy.  Isn’t that the joke?”

	She moved toward me then stopped, as if she had forgotten whether she
meant to slap me or hold onto me.  “You bastard.”

	I nodded.  I had been called worse by better women.

	“I want you out by the first if you’re serious,” she said quietly.
“No games.  No more of this halfway bullshit.  If you’re leaving, then
leave.”

	I looked at her and thought that for all my complaints about her, she
was the only one in the room with any courage.  She had, with one
sentence, made reality out of my endless posturing.

	“Fine,” I said.  “By the first.”

	She turned away and went into the bedroom.  I heard drawers open and
close.  I remained on the couch with my cigarette, feeling neither
victorious nor sad.  Mostly what I felt was tired and mean and more
awake than I had in months.

	May 15.  Heather finally called this afternoon.  I had been out walking
and returned to find a message on the machine.  She said she was sorry
she hadn’t called sooner, that she had still been sick and sleeping a
lot and trying to keep herself together.  Keep herself together.  I
heard in those words the beginning of distance.  A woman only says such
things when she has begun withdrawing to some protected country where
you are not permitted to travel.

	I called her back and she answered on the second ring.

	“You sound better,” I said.

	“A little.”

	“You disappeared.”

	“I told you I was sick.”

	“Yes, I know, but you vanished too.”

	There was a pause.  I heard the faint sound of traffic behind her, then
the clink of something metallic.  Maybe she was standing outside work,
smoking, watching people go by.  For a second I could picture her
clearly and that made it worse.

	“I’ve been thinking,” she said.

	I hated those words.

	“About what?”

	“About us.  About you.”

	“Those are not the same thing.”

	“No, they’re not.”

	Another pause.  I could feel the conversation slipping into one of
those channels from which there is no easy recovery.  I tried to force
brightness into my tone.

	“Why don’t I come see you tonight?”

	“I don’t want you to.”

	I swallowed hard.  “Because you’re sick?”

	“Because I don’t know what you want from me.”

	That was almost funny.  I looked around the apartment as if the answer
might have been written on the furniture.  “I want to see you.”

	“No, I mean really.”

	I sat on the edge of the couch and rubbed my forehead.  “Really?  I
want to be with you.”

	She sighed.  “That isn’t true, Chris.  You want out of your life and
you think I’m the door.”

	I felt a strange coldness move through me.  The sentence was so exact
that for a moment I wanted to hate her for it.  I wanted her to be
incorrect, to be petty, confused, romantic, naive—anything but exact.

	“That’s not fair.”

	“It’s probably not.  But it’s what I think.”

	I stood and walked to the window.  The street below was dull and
featureless.  “So what now?”

	“I don’t know.  I just know I can’t carry all of this.”

	“All of what?”

	“Your girlfriend.  Your other women.  The way you look at me like I’m
supposed to mean everything all at once.”

	I did not answer.  She had seen too much, and quickly.

	“I like you,” she continued, “but I can’t be your rescue story.”

	That line ended it, not officially, not with a dramatic good-bye or a
door slam, but with a horrible little precision.  A rescue story.  Was
that how thin and obvious I had become?

	We talked another few minutes, but only on the surface of things.  We
did not say we were finished and we did not say we would meet soon.  We
simply let the conversation expire.  When I hung up, I had the feeling
that I had been gently refused by reality itself.

	May 18.  I went to see my parents over the weekend and asked them for
the money.  I built the speech on the drive out there, revised it on the
stairs, and delivered it to my mother in the kitchen while she was
drying dishes.  The story was not wholly false, which I suppose is why I
was able to tell it so smoothly.  Rhindy and I were not working.  I was
suffering.  I needed out.  I was trying to write.  I was trying to get
my life together.

	My mother looked at me in the way mothers do when they are weighing the
merits of a son against the evidence of his failures.  She asked if this
move was wise, if I had a job lined up, if I had thought it through.

	“No,” I said.  “Not completely.”

	“Then why now?”

	“Because if I don’t leave now, I won’t.”

	She put the dish towel down and stared at me.  “And this isn’t about
another woman?”

	I smiled weakly.  “It’s always about another woman.”

	She did not smile back.

	My father said very little.  He sat in the other room listening to the
television and occasionally muttering something about responsibility.
My brother, back in town and as useless as ever, asked whether the new
roommate was attractive.  I wanted to hit him with a lamp.

	By evening, my mother told me she could lend me the money if I paid it
back as soon as I was able.  I thanked her and felt both relieved and
sick.  I had, by charm and half-truth and fatigue, secured the means of
escape.  Yet driving back into the city, I did not feel free.  I felt
like a thief with a full tank.

	May 21.  Gale called twice yesterday and three times today.  I let the
machine get it each time.  On the last message she laughed and said she
knew I was home and should stop acting like such a prima donna.  She
said Billy had tried calling again and she nearly threw up just hearing
his voice.  Then she said she missed me, then she said she hated me, and
then the tape clicked off.

	I did not call her back.

	Rhindy and I have become eerily civil.  This is worse in some ways than
all the fighting.  She has begun speaking to me as though I were already
gone.  She asks if I need a box.  She asks if I’m taking the lamp.  She
asks if the books by the bed are mine or hers.  At night we move around
each other carefully, like tenants who have confused a lease with love.

	We still had sex one final time.  I suppose I should record it because
it happened, although it meant nothing more than inertia.  She came to
me after midnight in one of her old T-shirts, stood there silently until
I looked up, then sat beside me and put her hand on my thigh.  Neither
of us said anything.  It was tender only in the sense that a funeral can
be called graceful.  When it was over, she rolled away and pulled the
sheet up to her shoulder.

	“Will you at least try to be decent to me this week?” she asked.

	“I’ll try.”

	“That’s not much.”

	“It’s what I have.”

	She began to cry then, very softly, which almost undid me.  Not enough
to stay, but enough to make me ashamed.

	May 28.  I got the money together, every humiliating dollar of it.
What I had from work, what I borrowed, what I extracted from my parents,
what I kept in envelopes and under books and in jacket pockets.  I put
it all on the table and counted it twice.  It looked like a child’s
idea of a fortune.

	Janet phoned in the evening and said everything was still set for the
first.  She sounded cheerful, almost excited, as if we were embarking on
some bright collegiate adventure and not simply rearranging the scenery
of another gradual collapse.

	“You okay?” she asked.

	“Sure.”

	“You don’t sound sure.”

	“I’m moving, Janet.  I don’t know what sure sounds like.”

	She laughed and told me not to worry, that once I was out things would
be different.  I had heard that phrase from too many mouths lately.
Different.  Better.  Freer.  Cleaner.  As if geography could absolve the
soul.

	Later that night I walked alone to the beach.  The air was cold and the
water black.  I tried to think of Heather and found I could only think
of the way she had said rescue story.  I tried to think of Rhindy and
could only think of the apartment with my absence already built into it.
I tried to think of Ninety-Nine and for one fleeting second I saw her
again, not on the sand beside me but farther down the shoreline,
motionless, as if waiting to see whether I would finally understand
something.

	I wanted to ask her if all this movement meant anything.  If leaving
counted as transformation.  If I had done enough to justify the pain I
had caused.  But she did not come closer.  She only remained there in my
mind—composed of moonlight, distance, and a promise no human being had
ever managed to keep.

	June 1.  We moved the boxes in two trips.  It was overcast on the
peninsula, the kind of weather that makes every house seem temporary. 
Janet was in good spirits, arranging books before the bed was even set
up, talking about curtains and coffee and where we might put the little
table by the window.  I brought in the last box and set it down in what
was now my room.  My room.  The phrase sounded theatrical, almost
embarrassing.

	The place was quiet.  No Rhindy in the kitchen.  No notes on the table.
No expectation humming in the walls.  I stood in the center of the room
and waited for the rush of relief that had been promised by every nerve
in my body these last weeks.  It did not come.

	What arrived instead was a vast and measured stillness.  I was out.  I
had done it.  I had extracted myself from one life and carried my books,
my typewriter, my clothes, my cigarettes, my bad habits, my fantasies,
my guilt, and my mind into another room in another town.  Yet there I
was, intact in all the ways I least wanted to be.

	Janet knocked on the open door holding two cups of coffee.

	“Well?” she said.  “How does freedom feel?”

	I took the cup from her and looked around at the undecorated walls, the
window, the boxes still half-opened on the floor.

	“Quiet,” I said.

	“That’s all?”

	“For now.”

	She smiled and left me alone.  I set the coffee on the windowsill and
sat down at the typewriter.  Outside, the afternoon hung in a grey haze.
There was no ocean here, at least not in sight, but I could feel the old
pull of distance.  Somewhere Rhindy was returning from work to an
apartment without me.  Somewhere Heather was living her life beyond the
weight of my projections.  Somewhere Gale was talking herself into or
out of another calamity.  And somewhere beyond all of them, beyond the
women and the rooms and the miles and the filth and the wanting, there
was still Ninety-Nine, not as a woman I could hold but as the shape of
everything I had failed to become.

	I rolled a page into the machine and pressed my fingers to the keys.
The room was quiet enough to hear myself think, which is not always a
blessing.  I no longer had any excuses—not Rhindy, not the apartment,
not the noise of the city, not the tyranny of needing to leave.  I had
my freedom, such as it was.  And the terrible thing about freedom is
that, once granted, it expects an answer.

	I struck the first key.

	For a long while I sat there listening to the single line I had made
and the silence around it.  Then I looked toward the window and in the
reflection, for the briefest moment, I thought I saw a woman with dark
red hair standing behind me, smiling not with promise but with knowing.
When I turned, there was no one there.

	I faced the page again.

	The only thing left to do was begin.
