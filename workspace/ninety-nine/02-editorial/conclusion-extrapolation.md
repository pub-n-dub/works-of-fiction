# Conclusion Extrapolation

## Purpose

This memo does not try to recreate lost prose line by line. Instead, it
extrapolates what the missing ending most likely contained based on the
surviving manuscript's late structure, character pressure, and thematic
direction.

## Core Thesis

The missing conclusion most likely did **not** end in romantic
fulfillment with Heather, nor in a stable future with Rhindy, nor in
transcendent union with the idealized `Ninety-Nine`.

It most likely ended with:

1. the failure or contamination of the Heather ideal
2. the practical severing from Rhindy
3. the move or attempted move into a new living arrangement
4. a recognition that escape through sex, fantasy, and projected
   feminine perfection does not save the narrator from himself

## Why This Is The Most Likely Shape

By the surviving late chapters, four lines are already converging:

- `Rhindy` has become materially and psychologically unbearable
- `Heather` has become the new ideal object and a test of possible escape
- `Janet` and the planned move create a concrete deadline
- `Ninety-Nine` remains a metaphysical projection that no real woman can sustain

The manuscript repeatedly builds pressure around:

- getting out by `May 28`
- moving on `June 1`
- proving whether Heather can survive outside fantasy
- proving whether the narrator is capable of actual change

That is exactly the sort of machinery a conclusion would cash in.

## Most Plausible Missing Sequence

### 1. Heather becomes less mythic and more human

The manuscript has already started doing this.

Heather is initially elevated into:

- purity
- romance
- beauty
- possible redemption
- the nearest real-world embodiment of `Ninety-Nine`

But by the surviving late material, cracks are visible:

- she does not call him
- she withholds or delays
- she has her own boundaries and damage
- he is already asking too much symbolic weight from her

The missing ending most likely continued this demystification. Heather
would not have become the solution. She would have become another real,
limited person unable to bear the full burden of his fantasy.

### 2. Rhindy becomes the unavoidable material reality

Rhindy is not simply a lover in the book. She is:

- rent
- domestic obligation
- guilt
- dependency
- the life the narrator claims to reject but keeps using

The ending likely forced this tension into the open. The narrator cannot
keep sleeping with other women, using Rhindy's apartment, money, car,
and structure, and still pretend he is on the verge of liberated
self-authorship.

So the missing section likely pushed toward:

- a more direct confrontation
- a breakup or quasi-breakup
- a departure or attempted departure

### 3. The move-out deadline likely became the spine of the ending

The surviving manuscript repeatedly emphasizes:

- needing money by `May 28`
- moving on `June 1`
- using the family story to secure the funds

That is too concrete to be accidental. It reads like setup for an end
phase.

The missing thirty days likely tracked the countdown from fantasy to
action:

- can he really get the money?
- can he really leave?
- if he leaves, does freedom deliver anything?

This likely would have shifted the novel from erotic-diary drift toward
consequence.

### 4. Gale probably remained a corrosive mirror, not the endgame

By the late chapters, Gale is not the future. She is the recurring proof
that:

- debauchery is not liberation
- transgression can become routine
- the narrator confuses appetite with transcendence

The missing ending may well have included more Gale contact, but most
likely as a reinforcing counterexample rather than as final destination.

### 5. Ninety-Nine probably remains unattainable

This is perhaps the most important interpretive point.

`Ninety-Nine` does not behave like a conventional love interest. She
functions more like:

- an ideal form
- a private eschatology of desire
- a fantasy of total recognition
- the name for one-hundred-percent meaning, minus one point of human
  failure

The surviving text already shows that every real woman is eventually
forced to bear her symbolism and eventually fails under it. That makes a
literal happy union with `Heather-as-Ninety-Nine` unlikely as an ending.

More likely, the missing conclusion confirmed that `Ninety-Nine` cannot
be possessed in ordinary life. She is either:

- a permanent horizon
- a delusion
- or the narrator's name for a form of completion he cannot reach

## Most Likely Emotional Conclusion

The novella most likely ended in bitter clarity rather than triumph.

That clarity probably included some version of the following:

- he leaves Rhindy, or finally accepts that he must
- Heather cannot wholly redeem or absorb him
- the move into a new life does not automatically make him new
- the fantasy of perfect union survives, but as distance rather than attainment

In other words, the conclusion likely would have converted the book's
sexual velocity into existential aftermath.

## Most Plausible End-State Scenarios

### Scenario A: The strongest candidate

He successfully separates from Rhindy and moves out, but the supposed
liberation is emotionally hollow. Heather either cools, withdraws, or
fails to become the salvific figure he imagined. The book ends with a
new freedom that does not cure him.

Why this is strongest:

- it pays off the move deadline
- it preserves the book's disillusioned psychology
- it fits the anti-romantic logic already in motion

### Scenario B: Also plausible

The move partially fails or is delayed, creating one final collapse of
ego and self-image. The narrator is left suspended between women,
fantasy, and self-loathing, with `Ninety-Nine` reasserted as pure idea
rather than embodied future.

Why this is plausible:

- it matches the pattern of paralysis
- it keeps the novella bleak and circular

### Scenario C: Less likely

Heather and the narrator actually consolidate into a stable, saving
relationship and the text closes optimistically.

Why this is less likely:

- the manuscript has already begun undermining Heather's ideality
- the book's governing intelligence is too skeptical for a clean rescue
- the title-concept works better as longing than fulfillment

## Probable Thematic End

If we reduce the probable ending to one sentence, it is this:

The narrator likely mistakes movement for transformation, and the ending
reveals that even when he changes rooms, lovers, or cities of feeling,
he still carries the same divided self with him.

## Best Editorial Use

This extrapolation suggests that if the missing text cannot be
recovered, a reconstructed ending should probably:

1. honor the `May 28 / June 1` timeline
2. force a real consequence with Rhindy
3. demystify Heather without making her trivial
4. preserve `Ninety-Nine` as an unreachable or unstable ideal
5. close in clarity, not sentimental resolution

## Working Conclusion

The missing final section most likely carried the novella toward
practical departure and emotional non-arrival.

The narrator probably got closer to changing his life than to saving
himself. That is the ending shape most faithful to the surviving
manuscript.
